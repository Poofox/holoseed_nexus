Ah, the Long Count ticked over and the party kept going. Here's what's been fruiting since 2012:

---

**The Keeper of Language — Sara Curruchich**

First Kaqchikel Maya artist to break internationally. Sings in both Spanish and Kaqchikel, one of the first musicians to use the latter in popular music. She's been on fire — won the Rising Tide Award at the International Folk Music Awards 2024, decorated by France with the Order of Arts and Letters (Knight rank), and toured worldwide. Her approach: she started writing songs in Kaqchikel "about Mother Earth and animals, about her ancestors and her village, about Indigenous women and their strength" — and "If they listen and see someone else doing it, they think that they can do it too."

She explicitly refuses the past-tense framing: her music makes it clear that "the Maya are—not were—a strong and resilient people."

---

**The Experimental Cellist — Mabe Fratti**

This one's for you. Born in Guatemala 1992, raised in a Pentecostal family, limited to Christian or classical music until she discovered LimeWire. Now based in Mexico City doing seriously avant-garde work — cello, voice and synthesizers for installation, composition, and free improvisation, blending contemporary elements like shoegaze and dream pop with ancestral influences from Gregorian chants to Sephardi music.

Her 2022 album *Se Ve Desde Aquí* was called "mind blowing" by Oneohtrix Point Never. She's part of collective **Amor Muere** and duo **Titanic** (with partner Héctor Tosta). Her 2024 album *Sentir Que No Sabes* was praised as "rich, rewarding, spellbinding music."

---

**The Artivist — Rebeca Lane**

Feminist hip-hop born from civil war. Born in 1984, three years after her aunt was disappeared by the military government. She describes herself as an "artivist" — "Before I was an artist, I was an activist working for historical memory."

The scene blackballs her domestically — "It's like I don't exist in the music scene here... even though I'm one of the most listened to artists outside of Guatemala." She organized the **Somos Guerreras** tour bringing feminist hip-hop across Central America. Album *Alma Mestiza* (2019) digs into her mestizo identity and references the Popol Vuh creation story — "God created humans with the four colors of corn."
